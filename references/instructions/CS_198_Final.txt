Pair Trading with a Continuous Sizing Strategy
Robert Yang
Abstract
Pairs trading is a trading strategy first used in the 1980s performed on two related1
securities. When the spread (which can have different mathematical definitions2
depending on the model) widens, the outperforming security is shorted and the3
underperforming security is longed. The model effectively bets that the spread4
between the two securities will go back down. In this paper, we simulate a pair5
trading strategy performed on KO and PEP (Coca Cola and Pepsi) trained on data6
from 2004 to 2013 and tested on data from 2014 to 2022.7
1 Examination of Data8
In this section, we will introduce some pair trading theory and test that theory on KO and PEP to see9
if they are a good target for pair trading.10
1.1 Cointegration test11
Usually, pair trading is performed on securities which have a high degree of cointegration, which12
means a linear combination of their time series is relatively stationary. A stationary time series13
regularly goes back to or hovers around a specific value. For trading purposes, this is useful because14
this linear combination is predictable; when it’s below the mean value, we can bet that it will15
eventually increase and vice versa. This allows us to make money with trades because it’s easy to set16
up our positions to profit from this: simply a long on the underperforming stock and a short on the17
outperforming one, as is done for pair trading.18

4 Continuously Sized Position Strategy55
In this section, we will test an alternative model, invented by the authors, that continuously sizes our56
position based on the spread.57
4.1 Theory58
We make a new position at the close of each day (by trading after-hours or right before the close) and59
our trade size will be proportional to the z-score of the spread.60
3
The logic here is that our spread is mean reverting, so consider what happens if the spread follows61
a mean reversion sequence a = (0 , 0.1, 0.2, ..., 1, 0.9, 0.8, ..., 0), where each spread value is at the62
close of each day. Then our position sizes based on day i is proportional to ai and we make money63
if ai+1 (the close price of the next day) is lower and lose money if it’s higher (since we expect the64
positive z-score spread to fall back to the mean). Thus, our return for the position started on day i65
will be c(ai+1 − ai)ai for some constant c. Based on this formula and the sequence, our return will66
be 0.1c(−0 − 0.1 − 0.2 − . . . − 0.9 + 1 + 0.9 + . . . + 0.1) = 0 .1c(1). This is equivalent to if we had67
started our position when the spread had z-score 1 and ended it when the spread returned to the mean.68
Note that 1 was the arbitrary maximum of the sequence, so this strategy effectively gave the same69
return as if our previous strategy was magically able to detect when the spread time series would70
locally peak, which is the best time to start a position.71
The issue is the spread clearly wouldn’t follow such a nice sequence in practice, but the general idea72
still holds: because our position size has magnitude proportional to the spread z-score of the previous73
day, our positions will be slightly larger when the spread reverts to the mean than when the spread74
strays away from the mean, generating a net profit.75
